Our Journey

Meet Layla

Born in 2022, Layla—affectionately known as Luli—is an absolute light. She rocks her very own pair of pink glasses and is completely obsessed with her favorite children's book, A Flat for Rent (Dira Lehaskir). To her mothers, Tamar and Eden, she is perfect.

But when Layla was just five months old, Tamar and Eden sensed that something wasn't quite right. They embarked on an exhausting medical journey, searching for answers. After all standard diagnostic scans came back completely normal, they were referred for extensive genetic sequencing.

When the results arrived, their reality changed forever. Layla was diagnosed with Xia-Gibbs Syndrome (XGS)—an ultra-rare, non-hereditary genetic condition caused by a disruption in the AHDC1 gene.

Today, Project Luli is driven by fierce love and a refusal to just wait for a miracle. We are racing against time, driving and funding the breakthrough research needed to find one.

The Medical Facts

Understanding Xia-Gibbs Syndrome (XGS)

Xia-Gibbs Syndrome is an ultra-rare neurodevelopmental condition caused by mutations in the AHDC1 (AT-Hook DNA Binding Motif Containing 1) gene. The AHDC1 protein plays a crucial role in key interactions within the cell nucleus, regulating gene expression and maintaining proper chromatin structure.

When a mutation disrupts this gene, the cell lacks structural control of key developmental targets, leading to a complex array of global developmental delays, severe hypotonia (low muscle tone), sleep issues, and other physical anomalies.

Currently, there is no cure and no specific therapeutic strategy to treat the root cause of XGS. We are determined to change that.

Scientific Endeavors

Pioneering the Path to a Cure

We are not waiting for a miracle; we are funding the science to create one. Project Luli actively collaborates with top-tier scientists to understand the pathogenic mechanisms of AHDC1 mutations and discover viable therapeutic options.

Principal Investigator

Prof. Miguel Weil

Department of Cell Research & Immunology, Tel Aviv University

Prof. Weil brings extensive expertise in the molecular signaling pathways of the developing nervous system, neurodegenerative diseases, and stem cell differentiation.

  • Leading a breakthrough initiative to develop a phenotypic assay for drug screening.
  • Utilizing patient-derived fibroblast cell models to identify potential therapeutic candidates.
  • Focusing on high-throughput screening to test existing libraries of FDA-approved compounds.
Research Partner

Dr. Lior Dor

Research Associate, ALYN PARC

Dr. Dor investigates personalized approaches for rare neurological disorders. He holds a PhD in Animal Sciences from the Hebrew University of Jerusalem and completed a postdoctoral fellowship studying induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSC).

  • Partnering to study patient-derived iPSCs to model neurological aspects of XGS.
  • Bridging the gap between molecular genetic findings and clinical applications.
  • Coordinating cell model development in collaboration with Prof. Weil.
